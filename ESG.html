Regression Report on ESG Components -Analysis of Indian Companies

1. Introduction

This report investigates the impact of ESG components—specifically carbon emissions, energy consumption, and employee turnover on the financial performance of Indian companies, measured through ROA and ROE. Using regression analysis, the study evaluates the significance of these factors while also considering the influence of industry type and geographic location, offering a comprehensive view of how environmental and social dimensions shape corporate outcomes in the Indian context.

2. Data Exploration

The data is collected and documented based on information published by the companies in their Annual Reports and Business Sustainability Reports.

Data of 12 Indian companies is taken with 51 observations in total.

3.1 Regression Results for ROA

  • Intercept = 10.85 - Baseline ROA when EC, CE, and ETO are zero.

  • EC, CE, and ETO - All p-values > 0.05 -Not statistically significant.

  • R² = 0.019 - Only ~1.9% of variation in ROA is explained - Model is weak

    Conclusion: ROA is not strongly related to EC, CE, or ETO in your data.

3.2 Regression Results for ROE

  • Intercept = 22.91 - Baseline ROE.

  • ETO (Employee Turnover) - Significant (p = 0.0195).

  • Negative coefficient = higher employee turnover reduces ROE.

  • EC, CE - Not significant.

  • R² = 0.112 - Model explains ~11% of variation.

    It is still weak, but slightly better than ROA model.

Conclusion

ROE is somewhat affected by ETO (negatively), while EC and CE don’t show meaningful impact in this data set.

Multicollinearity (VIF)

All VIF < 2 -No multicollinearity issues.

So, overall

  • Employee turnover (ETO) has a negative effect on ROE, but not on ROA.

  • Carbon emissions (CE) and energy consumption (EC) don’t show significant relationships with either ROA or ROE (at least with your sample size & data).

4.Key Findings

4.1 Core financial variables

  • log_EC (Energy Consumption, logged) - Not significant in both models.

  • log_CE (Carbon Emissions, logged) - Significant negative effect in both models:

    • ROA: −9.74 (p = 0.015)

      ROE: −13.74 (p = 0.019)
      Higher carbon emissions reduce financial performance.

    ETO (Employee-to-Rate ratio) - Not significant.

4.2 Industry effects

Classifieds, Clothing, Home Decor, Manufacturing, Pharmaceuticals have significant positive impact on ROA and ROE.

  • NBFC (Non-Banking Finance Company) - Positive but not significant.

4.3 Location effects

  • Bengaluru - Strong positive effect on both ROA (+35.9) and ROE (+57.9).

  • Bhubaneswar - Positive, significant for ROA (+18.3) and borderline for ROE (+22.7, p ≈ 0.054).

  • Mumbai - Negative, borderline for ROA (−27.9, p = 0.051), significant for ROE (−42.6, p = 0.044).

  • Hyderabad - Not significant.

Firms in Bengaluru & Bhubaneswar outperform, while Mumbai underperform relative to the baseline.

5.Conclusion

The overall analysis highlights that carbon emissions adversely affect company performance, exerting a negative influence on both ROA and ROE. Beyond environmental factors, structural aspects such as industry type and location play a significant role in shaping financial outcomes.

Among the selected industries, Home Decor, Clothing, Classifieds, and Pharma emerge as the strongest performers, reflecting sectors with better resilience or efficiency in managing ESG challenges.

Geographically, Banglore based companies demonstrate the best performance, while Mumbai-based firms lag behind, underscoring the importance of regional dynamics in determining ESG-driven financial performance.
